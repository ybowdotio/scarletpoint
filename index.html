About Scarlet Point

Scarlet Point is a research and storytelling initiative revealing the ontological unity between the broken body of Christ and the broken symmetry of the Standard Model. It weaves theology and cosmology into a single, coherent narrative—stretching from the birth of the universe to you reading this sentence..

The Book: Jailbreaking Christ

A radical theological hypothesis: that the Lamb slain from the foundation of the world is not metaphor but mechanism. That Christ's broken body is the structure of spacetime itself. Read excerpts, explore the implications, or download the critical analysis.

From the Back Cover

Epic discoveries in the twentieth century led physicists into a creation story suggesting that the four forces and twelve building blocks of nature actually flow from a body that was broken at the beginning of time.

In the Bible, Christ is revealed to be "the Lamb slain from the foundation of the world," "the beginning of the creation of God," in whom we now "live and move and have our being" and "by whom all things consist."

Throughout his childhood, Eugene L. Ulrich was overtaken by a curiosity about three things: the nature of light, the force of gravity, and the crucifixion of Christ. Facing strong cultural and religious headwinds, Eugene pressed deeply into the heart of these matters, not knowing they would lead him to a startling and most beautiful discovery.

In this book, Eugene:

  • → Delves into his journey navigating the treacherous waters between two creation stories.
  • → Tangles with the dogmas within biblicism and scientism.
  • → Witnesses a most marvelous and beautiful reality emerge.

Within these pages, Eugene suggests the Universe we live in is actually the broken body of Christ. As Apostle Paul states, "And he is before all things, and by him all things consist" and most explicitly stated by Christ that the soil and stream he held in his hand during the Last Supper was indeed "my body which is broken for you!"

About the Author

Eugene L. Ulrich

Eugene Ulrich is an amateur telescope maker, astronomer, and a passionate disciple of Christ. He writes about his lifelong addiction to exploring the meaning of life, especially the interface between Christ and the Cosmos.

Raised by Mennonite missionary parents in Costa Rica and sporting only an 8th-grade formal education, Ulrich lives in a love/hate relationship with the orthodoxies within science and religion.

He is happily married to his childhood sweetheart of 32 years and boasts 9 kids and 12 grandkids.

Ulrich’s passion is to expose a deep connection between what Christ says about himself and what scientists say about the Universe.

The Thesis

The central claim of Jailbreaking Christ is that the universe is the broken body of the Son—not metaphorically, but ontologically and structurally. The same broken symmetry that gives rise to mass, force, and field in the Standard Model is the very essence of Christ’s “broken body” described in Scripture.

Christ is not merely a historical figure crucified by men, but the cosmic Lamb—slain from the foundation of the world, not in time, but as time. (Think — "Alpha & Omega") The crucifixion is not just an event; it is the unveiling of a deeper reality that already upholds all things through its wound.

To “jailbreak” Christ is not to abandon faith, but to free it—from the confines of chapter and verse, dogma and doctrine.

This is an invitation to behold the Lamb through the stereoscopic lens of physics and scripture, and to recognize that creation itself is His body broken — the very structure of sacrifice, not the stage.

Critical Analysis

1. Clarity and Coherence

The analysis finds Ulrich's core claim—that the "broken" cosmos mirrors Christ's broken body—to be articulated with passionate clarity. The argument weaves personal narrative, scripture, and science into a generally accessible and coherent, if ambitious, thesis.

“Readers who accept his premises will find the thesis imaginative and coherent, whereas skeptics might view it as an extended metaphor pushed to its limits.”
2. Scientific Basis and Accuracy

Ulrich grounds his thesis in key concepts from modern physics, like broken symmetry and the Standard Model. The analysis concludes that he generally describes these ideas accurately for a lay audience, using them as a foundation for his theological interpretation.

“Physicists would agree with most of Ulrich's scientific descriptions (Big Bang, symmetry breaking, Higgs, etc.), but they would not endorse the theological conclusions drawn from them.”
3. Theological & Exegetical Foundations

The project is as much theological as it is scientific, centering on a highly cosmological Christology and a reimagining of atonement as an ongoing, cosmic reality. The analysis notes this is both innovative and a departure from some orthodox positions.

“Ulrich's Christology is bold but heterodox in places... He effectively transposes the Eucharist to a cosmic key: just as Christians believe they partake in Christ's broken body... Ulrich sees all creatures as literally partaking in Christ's broken cosmic body...”
4. Critiques and External Responses

The analysis explores potential critiques, noting that scientists might view the theological claims as an unfalsifiable interpretive layer, while theologians might challenge the departure from key orthodox doctrines and the allegorical use of scripture.

“Theologians would caution that Ulrich challenges orthodoxy on key points... Scientists would caution that Ulrich's theological overlay is not derived from scientific method and could mislead if taken as science.”
5. Integration and Coherence

The book's most striking aspect is its earnest attempt to integrate science and theology into a single framework. The analysis finds it to be a highly original, largely coherent, and imaginative synthesis that models a fearless engagement with both fields.

“As an interdisciplinary work... Jailbreaking Christ seems effective in communicating complex ideas in story and metaphor... it sparks wonder, invites further exploration, and models a fearless engagement with both Scripture and science.”
6. Atonement as Dynamic Reality

One of the most profound and beautiful aspects of Jailbreaking Christ is its rethinking of atonement—not as a past-tense legal transaction, but as a dynamic, ontological reality. Ulrich presents the broken body of Christ as the sustaining center of the cosmos: not only giving substance to creation, but also infusing every moment with the miracle of free will, the possibility of repentance, and the ongoing offer of redemption. Atonement, in this view, is not an event locked in time, but the very structure that holds time together. It is the ever-flowing act of divine self-giving through which reality — and moral agency — exists at all..

“Rather, Christ is the cosmic vine from which we the branches are drawing not only our physical life but the full wealth of redemption and salvation. This ever-present nourishing stream of his atoning and life-giving blood is a universal river flowing through the very fabric of the Cosmos.”
